Dr. A. P. J. Abdul Kalam was born on 15 October 1931 in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu.
He came from a simple family, and his father was a boat owner while his mother was a homemaker.
Despite financial struggles, Kalam was a bright and hardworking student from his childhood.
He sold newspapers as a boy to support his education and family.
His interest in science and flight began at an early age.
He studied physics at St. Joseph’s College in Tiruchirappalli.
Later, he pursued aerospace engineering at the Madras Institute of Technology.
His teachers noticed his sharp mind and encouraged him to pursue research.
After graduation, he joined the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO).
Soon, he moved to the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO).
At ISRO, Kalam played a major role in India’s first Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III).
The SLV-III successfully launched the Rohini satellite in 1980.
This made India one of the few countries with satellite launching capability.
Kalam’s leadership and vision were deeply respected by his colleagues.
Later, he worked on the Integrated Guided Missile Development Program (IGMDP).
He became the key figure behind India’s Agni and Prithvi missiles.
For these achievements, he was called the “Missile Man of India.”
In 1998, he contributed to India’s nuclear tests at Pokhran-II.
These tests made India a strong nuclear power.
He always believed that science and technology should serve society.
He promoted education, rural development, and youth empowerment.
His famous vision was to make India a developed nation by 2020.
He shared his thoughts in his popular book India 2020.
Kalam also wrote Wings of Fire, his inspiring autobiography.
His books continue to motivate students across the world.
In 2002, Abdul Kalam became the 11th President of India.
He was known as the “People’s President.”
He lived a simple life even while in the Rashtrapati Bhavan.
He was loved for his humility, kindness, and wisdom.
After completing his term in 2007, he returned to teaching.
He taught at institutions like IIM Shillong, Anna University, and others.
He believed that students are the real strength of India.
He often said that dreaming big is the first step to success.
His speeches were full of hope, positivity, and vision.
Kalam always encouraged youth to pursue science and innovation.
He was awarded the Bharat Ratna, India’s highest civilian honor, in 1997.
He also received Padma Bhushan and Padma Vibhushan awards earlier.
He never married and dedicated his entire life to the nation.
On 27 July 2015, he was delivering a lecture at IIM Shillong.
During the speech, he collapsed due to a cardiac arrest.
He passed away at the age of 83, leaving millions in sorrow.
His funeral was attended by leaders, citizens, and students from across the country.
Even after his death, he remains a symbol of knowledge and inspiration.
People remember him as a scientist, teacher, leader, and dreamer.
His life showed that success comes from hard work and honesty.
He taught that true wealth is not money, but values and service.
He believed India’s strength lies in its youth and education.
Dr. Kalam’s life continues to inspire generations of Indians.
He will always be remembered as the “Missile Man” and the “People’s President.”
